# WORLD.md — Один Семестр: World Specification

## The town of Малинов (Malinov)

Malinov is not special. That is its defining quality.

It is not ugly. It is not charming in a postcard way. It is lived-in — which means it has grocery kiosks next to century-old wooden houses, a fountain in the park that works in summer and is turned off in October, a market that has been in the same location since the Soviet era, a cafe that has not updated its menu since 2011, and a train station that connects Malinov to the rest of Russia in a way that mostly benefits people who want to leave.

Population: approximately 18,000.
University: yes, small, mostly engineering and economics.
Industry: light manufacturing, some agriculture, the train line.
Notable feature: nothing. That is the point.

The player will, by the end of the semester, find this town beautiful. Not because it became beautiful. Because they learned to see it.

---

## Visual and atmospheric principles

**Time of year:** Late August through December. The game begins in late summer warmth and moves toward the first real Russian winter. The light changes. The trees change. The pace of the town changes.

**Color palette:** Warm base with lived-in saturation. Amber and ochre for autumn. Soft greys and muted blues for late autumn. Never grimy. Never dark for darkness's sake. The contrast between the warmth of indoor spaces and the cooling outdoor world is part of the world's character.

**Ambient detail:** Each location has background characters — people who pass through, sit at tables, browse stalls, wait for trains. They do not speak. They are not interactive. But they make the world feel populated without adding cognitive load.

**Day/night cycle:** The game runs on a soft time-of-day system. Morning, afternoon, early evening. Not real-time. Time advances through player actions and missions. NPCs have different energy and dialogue at different times of day (documented per-NPC in STORY.md).

## Location 1: Apartment building (Жилой дом)

**The place:**
A five-story Khrushchev-era apartment block. Concrete exterior, slightly warmer than you'd expect from photos — someone planted window boxes. Narrow entrance hall. A notice board inside the door with handwritten notes. The elevator is broken and has been for six months. The stairwell smells of cooking.

The player's apartment is on the third floor. It is small, functional, slightly shabby. It is theirs.

**The feel:**
Domestic. Close. This is where the player sleeps, and the NPCs here know it. There is a kind of intimacy in this location that the others do not have — these people are neighbors, not customers or strangers.

**8+ distinct visit reasons across the full game:**

1. First arrival — finding the apartment, meeting Galina Ivanovna, establishing home base
2. Daily return — player passes through regularly; Galina Ivanovna is often in the hallway or at her door
3. Missions originating here: deliver jam to apartment 7, check the post, the Dmitry Petrovich noise report
4. Styopan encounters: he stops player in the hall, on the stairs, near the mailboxes
5. Vocabulary catch-up: player can return to Galina Ivanovna to practice or reinforce anything from earlier chapters; she will always engage
6. Cross-location return moments: player comes back from the park / cafe / market; NPCs react to what the player has done
7. Chapter test preparation: the player reviews their journal notes here; the apartment is where the chapter test is conceptually "prepared"
8. Chapter 4 anchor: this is the last place player sees before the trouble and the first place they return to after it's resolved; both scenes are significant
9. Seasonal changes: the apartment and building exterior change visually across the semester; Galina Ivanovna comments on this

**NPC cross-references active here:**
- Galina Ivanovna references Artyom (park), Fatima (market), mentions Lena with skepticism
- Styopan references Artyom, Lena at the cafe, the train station

**Vocabulary anchored here:** A1 pure — greetings, introductions, numbers 1-10, basic politeness, "I live at...", apartment and building words

## Location 2: Park (Парк)

**The place:**
The town's central park. Old trees, a path that circles a small fountain, chess tables in one section, benches along the main path. A kiosk near the entrance sells drinks and a newspaper. The park is small enough to feel like a known space after two visits and large enough to have corners that feel private.

In late August: green and warm, dappled light, the smell of grass. By October: leaves and a chill. The visual shift is part of the world.

**The feel:**
Open. The first open space the player has access to. After the enclosed apartment building, the park feels like the town has expanded. It is a breathing space — slower, less transactional than the cafe or market.

**8+ distinct visit reasons across the full game:**

1. First unlocking visit — player follows Galina Ivanovna's recommendation; meets Artyom
2. Artyom missions: find the chess players, deliver a message, help him with an English translation
3. Tamara Andreyevna missions: find her glasses, describe the fountain, vocabulary practice as natural conversation
4. Directional exploration: player practices navigation within the park layout — the game's first physical orientation challenge
5. Cross-chapter return: player passes through the park to reach the cafe and market (it is on the route); Artyom may be there, may not be
6. Atmospheric visit: no mission — player sits on a bench, a small reflective beat (the journal fills in)
7. Seasonal visits: the park looks and feels different in October than in August; NPCs reflect this
8. Late-game return: player has more language now; revisiting Artyom with greater ability changes the conversation fundamentally
9. Cross-reference hub: Artyom mentions or connects almost every other NPC; the park is where the town's social web becomes visible to the player

**NPC cross-references active here:**
- Artyom references Galina Ivanovna, Fatima, Lena, Konstantin
- Tamara Andreyevna references Galina Ivanovna and Artyom's mother

**Vocabulary anchored here:** Directions, adjectives, colors, simple present tense, question words, weather, basic descriptions of people and places

## Location 3: Cafe (Кафе)

**The place:**
A small cafe on the corner of the main street. Lena is behind the counter. There are six tables. A chalkboard menu on the wall — some items have prices, some are blank (Lena will tell you). The window seat is Boris Gennadyevich's. There is a small terrace outside that is used in warm weather.

Warm colors. Wood surfaces. The coffee machine is old and loud and excellent.

**The feel:**
Transactional at first, personal quickly. The cafe requires the player to perform — they have to order, pay, interact — in a way the apartment and park do not. But the warmth of the space and the way Lena runs it makes failure feel low-stakes.

**8+ distinct visit reasons across the full game:**

1. First visit — player attempts to order; Lena teaches the vocabulary through the transaction
2. Ordering missions: fetch an order for an outside table, learn a regular customer's order
3. Boris Gennadyevich encounters: finding him, talking to him, the newspaper missions
4. Price and payment practice: numbers 10-100 anchored here through multiple return visits
5. Cross-chapter bridge: player comes back in Chapter 3 with new vocabulary and can order more confidently — Lena notices
6. Social hub: Lena knows everyone; player learns things about other NPCs through cafe conversations
7. Rainy-day / slow-day variant: the cafe is the place that changes most with the time of day and weather; different visit contexts feel meaningfully different
8. Late-game comfort stop: player comes in not because of a mission but because it has become a routine; this is a deliberate small beat
9. Chapter 4 scene: player comes in under stress; Lena notices; the exchange is different

**NPC cross-references active here:**
- Lena references Artyom (charges him half price), Galina Ivanovna, Fatima, Konstantin
- Boris Gennadyevich references Tamara Andreyevna (from the park), Konstantin

**Vocabulary anchored here:** Ordering, polite requests, drinks/food names, prices, numbers 10-100, accusative case in context, "how much does this cost"

## Location 4: Market (Рынок)

**The place:**
An open-air market in a covered arcade — a long row of stalls under a metal roof that keeps out most of the rain. Produce stalls, household goods, a few stalls with clothing and shoes. Fatima's stall is roughly in the middle on the left. Misha's is further down on the right.

Crowded on weekday mornings, emptying by afternoon. The smells change by section — vegetables, spices, something mechanical from the far end.

**The feel:**
The most socially complex location so far. Multiple vendors, a crowd, things happening in the background. The player has to navigate to specific stalls, deal with multiple characters, make choices. It is a contained social challenge.

**8+ distinct visit reasons across the full game:**

1. First visit — finding Fatima's stall; first transaction with real stakes (she won't sell until you ask properly)
2. Shopping missions: buy a list, compare prices, fetch from a specific stall
3. Misha encounters: find the lost item, the translation mission, inventory count
4. Reinforcement: vocabulary from cafe and apartment reappears here in new contexts (numbers, colors, adjectives)
5. Cross-NPC evidence: Fatima mentions Galina Ivanovna, Artyom; the town's web tightens
6. Afternoon/rainy-day variants: the market changes texture at different times; near-close visits feel different
7. Late-game shopping: player can now conduct full transactions without prompting; this is tracked silently
8. Final chapter provision: Fatima sends player off with something before the station visit; a quiet story beat
9. Background observation: NPCs the player hasn't met are visible at other stalls; the world feels bigger than what's interactive

**NPC cross-references active here:**
- Fatima references Galina Ivanovna, Artyom, Lena
- Misha references Artyom, Fatima

**Vocabulary anchored here:** Produce and household item names, quantities, colors, quality adjectives, paying and receiving change, declining politely

## Location 5: Train station (Железнодорожный вокзал)

**The place:**
A small but dignified station — Soviet-era architecture, high ceilings, a main hall with wooden benches, a departures board, two ticket windows. Konstantin's is the main window. Nadya's is the kiosk to the side. A platform visible through the glass doors. One train per hour in each direction.

The station has a specific quality of light — it comes in through high windows and falls in wide shafts. It makes the station feel serious and a little cinematic.

**The feel:**
The first place that connects Malinov to the rest of Russia. Standing at the platform, you can see tracks that go somewhere else. This is where the register changes — the language here is formal, official, time-sensitive. The player has to be competent here, not just trying.

**8+ distinct visit reasons across the full game:**

1. Unlock visit — player arrives with a practical need; Konstantin and Nadya both engage
2. Schedule and ticket missions: checking train times, buying a ticket, finding the right platform
3. Helping a traveler mission — player acts as a translator/guide for someone more confused than them
4. Directional complexity: the station interior requires orientation language (platform numbers, windows, exits)
5. Nadya relationship: her character arc (Saint Petersburg aspiration) develops across multiple visits
6. Formal language training: the station is where ты/вы is made explicit; the player is prompted to use вы here
7. Time pressure variant: busy arrival period — shorter, faster interactions — player learns to be direct
8. Late evening variant: quiet station — Konstantin and Nadya are different when there are no trains; something more personal is available
9. Chapter 4 anchor: the station is central to the crisis and its resolution; at least two significant scene beats happen here

**NPC cross-references active here:**
- Konstantin references Galina Ivanovna, Boris Gennadyevich, Artyom
- Nadya references Artyom, Lena

**Vocabulary anchored here:** Time expressions, future tense, timetable reading, ticket vocabulary, directions in buildings, days of the week, formal request language

## Location 6: Police station (Полицейский участок)

**The place:**
A modest official building on a side street. Reception desk when you enter — Alina Sergeyevna. A waiting area with four chairs and a notice board covered in official papers. Viktor's office is through a door on the left. The building is clean, slightly institutional, not intimidating.

**The feel:**
Different from all other locations. This is formal Russia — paperwork, procedure, official language. The player must communicate clearly or nothing gets done. This is the final exam of the semester as a place, not a test room.

**8+ distinct visit reasons across the full game:**

1. Unlock visit — player comes with a real problem; the bureaucracy begins
2. Form-filling missions: the right form, in the right place, completed correctly
3. Delivering and confirming information: player must travel between this location and the station or market to gather or confirm details
4. Viktor relationship: develops from formal to marginally personal across multiple visits
5. Alina relationship: her dry humor only emerges across repeat visits — it rewards engagement
6. Formal register immersion: this is where the player consolidates formal Russian under real communicative pressure
7. Past tense narrative: telling the story of what happened; this is the primary past-tense practice context
8. Post-resolution visit: player returns to confirm things are resolved; a very different visit than the crisis visits
9. Case awareness anchor: genitive after negation (у меня нет документов) arises naturally in this context and is the first time the player becomes aware of case as a system

**NPC cross-references active here:**
- Viktor references Galina Ivanovna, Konstantin
- Alina references Galina Ivanovna (biscuits), Nadya (lost item visit), Viktor

**Vocabulary anchored here:** Formal register throughout, past tense narrative, document vocabulary, case awareness (genitive introduced in context), emergency and official phrases, formal greetings and farewells

## Seasonal arc

The game takes place across a semester: late August through mid-December.

**August/September (Chapters 1-2):**
Warm, green, long evenings. The park is in full summer. The cafe terrace is open. The market is at its fullest with late-summer produce. The player arrives in warmth and ease.

**October (Chapter 3):**
The leaves change. The terrace closes. The market shifts to root vegetables and preserves. The park is golden and a little melancholy. First cold evenings. Galina Ivanovna comments on this.

**November (Chapter 4):**
Bare trees. The fountain is turned off. The market is under heavier cover. The station is colder and the waiting room is fuller. The police station feels warmer by contrast — it has better heating.

**December (Final scenes):**
First snow possible. The town changes. The player's relationship to the town changes with it. Galina Ivanovna: "You'll miss this next year." Said as a fact, not a sentiment.

## What makes each location distinct as a place

**Apartment building:** Privacy and necessity. You live here. The stakes of every interaction are domestic and personal. The NPCs know you in a different way than anyone else — they see you on bad days and early mornings.

**Park:** The first outdoor freedom. Space and light. The social pressure is low — you can walk away. Artyom does not need anything from you. This makes the park the place where learning happens through choice rather than necessity.

**Cafe:** The first commerce. You have to perform here — order, pay, thank. Failure is visible (though mild). Success is satisfying. Lena's presence means the cafe is also the place where the player first feels genuinely seen and approved of by a peer-aged adult.

**Market:** The first complexity. Multiple vendors, a crowd, competing demands. The player cannot talk to everyone at once. The market teaches selectivity — who to trust, how to prioritize. Fatima's rule (ask properly or nothing) is the market's core lesson in non-linguistic form.

**Train station:** The first formality. The register changes and the player feels it. The stakes are higher — you can miss a train. Time matters here in a way it does not at the park. The station is also the first place that connects Malinov to the wider world, which makes it emotionally significant.

**Police station:** The end of the journey. Formal Russia at full force. The player has to marshal everything they know — vocabulary, register, past tense, patience — to navigate a real crisis in a language they are still learning. The fact that they can do it is the arc of the whole game made concrete.
